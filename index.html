Introduction

Project Overview

The Olympic Games provides a rare opportunity to study elite athletic performance across more than a century of competition, making them a compelling arena for understanding the relationship between physical attributes and competitive success. Track and Field is a particularly rich discipline because it spans an enormous range of event types (sprints, long distance marathons, jumping, and throwing) each placing very different demands on an athlete's body. Performance at the Olympic level is often viewed as a mix of talent, training, and physical traits; however, it is not always clear how correlated these measurable attributes relate to outcomes.

This project investigates the 2016 Rio Summer Olympics Track & Field competition with three central tasks: (1) describe the distribution of athletes across event categories by gender and medal status; (2) analyze whether physical metrics (age, height, weight, BMI) differ between medalists and non-medalists; and (3) identify which physical ranges are associated with above-average medal rates within each metric. Together, these visualizations help answer: Do physical attributes predict Olympic success, or does event specialization matter more?

Our analysis reveals that body composition is strongly event-driven but only weakly predictive of medal outcomes within any given event, suggesting that training, technique, and competitive strategy are the decisive factors. Check out this foundational research about how body dimensions relate to athletic performance, or explore this research article on BMI as a performance parameter for speed to learn more.

Dataset

Data Description

The data comes from the Kaggle dataset "120 Years of Olympic History: Athletes and Results" (heesoo37, 2018), which aggregates athlete-level records from the 1896 Athens Games through Rio 2016. The original dataset contains 271,116 rows and 15 columns describing athletes and their gender, age, height, weight, team, event, medals. We are using only data from the 2016 Track and Field events, reducing the data to 2508 rows and the same 15 columns.

The dataset contains both physical measurements and competition outcomes for every individual athlete, enabling direct comparison between medalists and non-medalists. However, it does not capture performance times or distances, training load, nationality-level resource disparities, or event qualification standards — all factors that may confound the physical-attribute–medal relationship.

Event Category Overview: Participation & Medal Rate

Takeaway

First, we observe the distribution of athletes across events. Sprints/Hurdles have the most athletes, while Combined Events have very few. There is generally equal participation between men and women in track and field events, though the side-by-side bar plot highlights how more men participate in Long Distance/Race Walking events. We also see that across all events, there is a 7.7% average medal rate. Long Distance/Race Walking has a very low medal rate, suggesting a competitive landscape with few opportunities to medal. On the other hand, Sprints/Hurdles and Throws events have above average medal rates. Despite having few athletes, the Combined Events also has a high medal rate - this could be attributed to all athletes on the winning teams sharing the Medalist status.

Design Notes

We used a grouped bar chart so men and women can be compared side by side in each event category without jumping between plots. The top buttons switch between athlete count and medal rate, which makes it easier to see how participation and outcomes do not always move together. In medal-rate mode, the dashed orange line marks the overall average (7.7%) so standout categories are easy to spot. Tooltips and click highlights add context without cluttering the chart.

BMI Distribution by Event Category

Takeaway

To dive deeper into the physical attributes of athletes, we look at the BMI distribution across events. Similar to height and weight, Throw athletes have a notably greater median BMI. We ordered the box plots so BMI patterns by event are easier to scan from low to high. This keeps medians, spread, and outliers visible at the same time. The All/Medalists/Non-Medalists toggle helps show where the distribution shifts when you isolate performance groups.

Medal Rate by Physical Metric Range

Takeaway

Finally, we look directly at medal rate across binned ranges of each physical metric. For age, athletes in the 28-33 range have the highest overall medal rate. This chart bins athletes by age, height, weight, or BMI and then compares medal rate across those bins. The dashed white line gives a quick baseline at the overall average (7.7%). Metric and gender controls let users answer focused questions without leaving the same panel.

Summary & Conclusions

What We Learned

Physical attributes cluster by event, not by medal status

Across all events, physical metrics have a weak correlation to an athlete's medal status. However, these attributes cluster strongly across event types. Throw athletes have the greatest heights, weights, and BMIs, while Long Distance/Race Walking athletes are shorter and lighter with low BMIs. Sprints/Hurdles and Jumps athletes fall in the middle. Within each event, medalists and non-medalists share similar body composition distributions, suggesting that at the elite level, athletes already self-select into events that suit their body type.

Age and BMI show the clearest signal for medal success

While height and weight show only a general upward trend in medal rate, age and BMI reveal more distinct patterns. Athletes who medal are most commonly in the 24–29 age range, with men peaking slightly earlier than women. For BMI, medal rate rises at both the low and high ends, reflecting the body type advantages specific to distance running and throwing events. Combined, these findings suggest that competitive experience paired with event-specific body composition offers the best chance of medaling.

Future work

Similar studies can be conducted on other Olympic games to follow how these trends progress over time. Further analysis could include performance metrics to add another dimension of comparison between physical attributes and event outcomes. Controlling for individual events rather than event categories would also provide further insight to whether physical attributes predict medal success.
